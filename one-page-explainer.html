Why the gap keeps widening — and the one thing that fixes it

What I've been working on, in one page.

Short version: the economy isn't broken because people are greedy. It's broken because money takes too long to get back to regular people — and that delay is what turns a steady, shared rise into a runaway gap. Fix the delay, and the gap can close without anyone having to lose.

The idea, as a shower

A shower with a slow tap is misery: you turn it up, nothing happens, you turn it up more — then it's scalding, you yank it back, now it's freezing. The delay is what makes it swing wildly. A responsive shower lets you settle into something comfortable. The economy is a shower with a decades-long delay between the money at the top and everyone else — and that delay is exactly what makes the gap swing open into a "K."

The equation — in plain words

K′ = αK − βC
C′ = κ·K(after delay τ) − γC
K = money at the top · C = money in people's hands
α how fast the top grows on its own (leverage) · β what it hands back · κ the share that cycles back · τ the delay before it does · γ what leaks away (interest, rent, inflation)

In a sentence, the math just says: the top grows fast on its own; some trickles back to people — but only after a long delay, and with a lot lost on the way.

We don't try to stop the top from growing (we leave α alone). We shrink one thing.

The one knob we turn: τ — the delay. Today it's decades (your 401(k) pays off at retirement, not this year). Long delay → a K. Short delay → a wave.

The fix: a stake you can actually use, soon

When you buy an iPhone, you help create Apple's value — but it all flows to shareholders, and your only share is stock you can't touch for 30 years. The idea: what if using or buying something gave you a small, real, soon-usable stake in the value you helped make? A loop that today takes decades would close in weeks. You're not waiting for charity or retirement — you're a participant in real time. (It can run on ordinary tools — co-ops, customer rewards, a fast public fund — no crypto required.)

"But wouldn't the people on top never allow it?"

This is the best objection — and it's right. The people at the top of the K benefit from the K and hold the power to keep it. Here's the honest answer, not a dodge:

  1. It's not take-from-the-rich. The whole economy still rises — we only bound the gap and the instability. A steady rising wave can reach a higher top than a funnel that keeps crashing. The pitch is "everyone richer, just less fragile," not "redistribute."
  2. The best path doesn't need their permission. Companies can give customers a stake because it makes them money — owner-customers are more loyal and grow the business. That routes around the blockade entirely.
  3. Even the top needs stability. Top-heavy economies crash, and crashes destroy the very wealth they're holding. Henry Ford paid workers enough to buy his cars because a broad base made him richer. COVID proved the system can flip the fast channel on when forced to.
  4. The honest concession: none of this is guaranteed. Entrenched power is a real headwind. The realistic route is the competitive/permissionless one — plus the occasional crisis that forces the issue — not generosity from the top. The point isn't "everyone will agree"; it's "here's the actual lever, and a way forward that doesn't require asking permission."

"Fix the delay, and the gap closes without anyone having to lose."
