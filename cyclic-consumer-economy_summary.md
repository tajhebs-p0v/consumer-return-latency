# Why the Economy Is a Funnel When It Could Be a Wave

### Fixing the K-shaped economy by speeding money back to the people who actually spend it

**By Taj Anderson** · Independent researcher · June 2026
*A plain-language companion to the working paper "Consumer-Return Latency as the Control Parameter Between K-Shaped Divergence and Cyclic Growth." Math, model, and data are in the white paper and the open-source repo ⟨URL⟩. Licensed CC BY 4.0 — share freely with credit.*

## The picture in one breath

Draw two lines starting at the same point. One curves steeply upward — that's the stock market, asset owners, the financial sector. The other stays nearly flat — that's the wages and savings of most households. The widening space between them is the **K-shaped economy**, and it is not a metaphor: in late 2025 the top 1% of U.S. households held a record ~31.7% of all wealth, roughly equal to the entire bottom 90% combined, and the top 10% own more than **87% of all stocks**. When markets rise, the gains land there first and fastest. Consumption itself has K-ed — the top 10% of earners now account for nearly **half of all consumer spending**.

This paper argues that the K is not destiny. It's the symptom of one fixable thing: **money takes too long to get back to consumers, and too much leaks on the way.**

## Why money pools at the top first

There's an 18th-century idea that explains the whole thing. Richard Cantillon noticed that **newly created money never enters evenly** — whoever is closest to where it's printed spends it first, at yesterday's prices, and captures the real gain. Everyone further away gets the money later, after prices have already risen, so they get a watered-down version. Today the people "closest to the printer" are banks, the financial sector, and asset holders. The consumer is structurally **far** from the printer.

So credit and stimulus enter at the top, inflate assets, and only **trickle** back to consumers — slowly, and after a toll. The classic "trickle-down" return path leaks badly: by the time value reaches the household, much of it has been skimmed by interest, rent, and inflation. Even the household's main link to the markets — a 401(k) — pays off **decades later**, at retirement. That's not a return loop; it's a return *delay*.

## The key idea: it's about speed, not just fairness

Most proposals to fix inequality argue about **fairness** and pay out **slowly** — an annual dividend from a national fund, a retirement account that matures in 40 years. Those help the *level* of inequality. But they barely touch the thing that actually drives the funnel shape: **latency** — how fast value cycles back to the people who turn it into demand.

Here's the intuition, and it's the same one behind every oscillating system in nature (a pendulum, a heartbeat, the sine wave from circular motion): **a feedback loop that returns energy quickly stays stable and bounded; the same loop with a long delay starts to swing wider and wider until it tears itself apart.** Engineers call the tipping point where a stable wobble becomes a runaway one a *bifurcation*. Economies have one too.

- When value returns to consumers **fast**, the economy behaves like a **rising wave** — it still cycles up and down, but it's bounded, and consumers rise *with* companies. A sine curve on an upward trend.
- When value returns **slowly** (today's setup), the same economy tips into a **widening funnel** — the swings get bigger, the top pulls away, and the gap forks open into a K.

Same economy. Same people. Same productivity. **Only the speed of the return loop is different** — and that one parameter decides whether you get a wave or a funnel. (The companion model shows this precisely: dial the delay up, and a perfectly stable system crosses a threshold and starts diverging.)

## The shift: put the consumer inside the value loop, in real time

If latency is the problem, the fix is defined by its goal: **shorten the loop.** Give consumers a stake in the value they help create that pays back **on the cadence of using and buying**, not on the cadence of retirement.

Think of it as the difference between:

- **Today:** you buy a company's product → the company's value rises → that value flows to Wall Street → maybe, decades later, a sliver reaches your 401(k). A loop measured in **years**.
- **Proposed:** you buy/use a product → you accrue a real, liquid claim on that value **now** → that spending power feeds the next turn of production. A loop measured in **weeks**.

The point isn't a new welfare check. It's **velocity**. The consumer stops being the last, most-diluted recipient and becomes a near-real-time participant — so the money cycles *through* the economy instead of pooling at the top and dribbling back.

## Is this new? Honestly — partly

It would be dishonest to claim this is invented from scratch, and over-claiming would get it laughed out of the room. The pieces have serious pedigree:

- The "money pools at the top first" mechanism is **Cantillon (1755)**.
- The "economy as an oscillating company-vs-consumer cycle" is **Goodwin's growth-cycle model (1967)** — literally a predator-prey wave.
- The "swings grow until they break" instability is **Minsky**.
- The "give people a capital stake and a dividend" idea has a crowded field: **Varoufakis's Universal Basic Dividend**, **Yang's Freedom Dividend**, **Altman's American Equity Fund**, the **Alaska Permanent Fund**, and the web3 **"ownership economy."**

**What's genuinely different** is narrow — and it's worth being honest, because over-claiming would sink it. Mainstream macro already knows that getting money to lower-income, high-spending households boosts demand (the "HANK" literature), and economists already link concentration to slower money circulation. Even "latency of money" exists as a technical term. So the unique slice is not any of those mechanisms. It's the *integration and the design rule*:
1. It treats inequality as a **stability/fragility problem**, and pins the regime (funnel vs. wave) on **one measurable knob — the speed of the return loop** — claiming that crossing a critical speed flips the whole system, not just changes the size of an effect.
2. It says **design the instrument by its speed**, not its fairness: a 401(k) is slow, an annual dividend is medium, a per-use consumer stake is fast — pick the fast one.
3. It ties value-return to the **act of participating** (a network principle), on rails left deliberately open.

That's a synthesis with a sharp, testable edge — enough for a viewpoint essay and an open-source working paper, **not** a finished theory ready for peer review. The honest gap is empirical: someone has to measure the actual speed of the loop and show that faster loops really do bend the K back into a wave.

## We already watched it happen, briefly, in 2020

This isn't only theory — there's a real episode that looks like the experiment you'd want to run. During COVID, the U.S. did the usual thing at the top (flood markets with cheap money → an asset boom for owners). But because of lockdowns, it *also* did something it almost never does: it sent money **directly and fast** to households — stimulus checks, boosted unemployment, the expanded Child Tax Credit. For a brief window, the slow loop became a fast one.

The result matched the prediction. Post-transfer income inequality fell to a **14-year low** in 2020; child poverty **dropped 41% in 2021** — and then, when the fast payments were switched off, it **jumped 85% in 2022** and inequality climbed right back. The tell: *market* inequality (before transfers) never stopped rising the whole time. The underlying funnel kept running — the fast channel just covered it up, and the moment you removed the channel, the cover came off.

Two honest notes. First, this proves the **lever** (speed of return), not the **vehicle** — those were emergency government checks, not the self-funding consumer stake the proposal imagines. Second, the same episode shows the **danger**: too much fast cash against locked-down supply helped produce the worst inflation in 40 years. That's the guardrail, not a refutation — speed has to be structural and matched to what the economy can actually produce.

## What could go wrong (because it can)

- **Inflation.** Speed money back to consumers faster than the economy can produce, and you get rising prices, not rising prosperity. The speed has to track real output.
- **Patient capital matters.** The "slow" money at the top isn't all extraction — long-horizon capital funds the R&D and infrastructure that fast consumer money won't. The goal is to shorten the *consumer's* loop, not to abolish long horizons.
- **It can be gamed.** Any "consumer stake" can be captured or turned into a marketing gimmick that entrenches incumbents.
- **It's a security.** A consumer "stock/dividend" runs straight into securities law and taxation — solvable, but not trivial.
- **Don't lean on any one technology.** The invariant is a *network principle* — value routes back fast to the people who generate it — and it works on **traditional rails** (cooperative patronage, customer equity, a fast-cadence public fund), **token rails**, or a **hybrid**. The "ownership economy" / crypto version is *one option, not the premise*; so far it has often concentrated ownership and added volatility, so it has to be earned. What matters is the *speed of the loop*, not the rails.

## The bottom line

The K-shaped economy looks like a law of nature. It isn't. It's what you get when the loop that returns value to consumers is too slow and too leaky. Speed that loop up — put consumers inside the value cycle in real time — and the funnel can become a wave: still cyclical, but bounded, rising, and shared.

The idea is publishable as an open viewpoint and a preprint today. To make it bulletproof, the next step isn't more theory — it's measuring the actual speed of the loop, and showing that economies with faster loops really do bend the K back into a wave.
